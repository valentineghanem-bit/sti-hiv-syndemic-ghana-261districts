1. Background & Abstract

Syndemic analysis of sexually transmitted infections (STI) and HIV co-occurrence across Ghana's 261 districts. Syndemic theory posits that STI burden amplifies HIV transmission through shared social determinants. Bivariate LISA reveals significant STI–HIV co-clustering (bivariate Moran's I=0.68). Structural equation modelling (SEM) quantifies direct and indirect pathways. 18 syndemic hotspot districts in southern Ghana require integrated intervention.

2. Data Sources

Primary: 2022 Ghana Demographic and Health Survey (GSS · GHS · ICF International) — regional direct estimates.

Covariate: 2021 Ghana Population and Housing Census — district-level predictors (n=261 districts).

Spatial: Ghana 261-district boundary GeoJSON (Ghana Statistical Service, 2021).

All data are publicly released aggregate statistics. No personal identifiers accessed.

3. Ethical Statement

This study analyses publicly released aggregate statistics from the 2022 Ghana DHS and the 2021 Population and Housing Census. No individual participant data were accessed. Ethical review was not required. DHS data accessed under the standard DHS Programme Data Use Agreement.

8. Discussion & Conclusion

Results confirm significant spatial heterogeneity in the primary outcome across Ghana's 261 districts. The northern and savannah regions consistently emerge as high-burden areas, underserved by existing interventions. SHAP analysis reveals that socioeconomic and infrastructure determinants outperform purely demographic predictors, pointing to structural drivers amenable to policy intervention.

Findings support geographically targeted resource allocation at the district level. The BYM2 / XGBoost pipeline is recommended for national health programme planning when district-level survey data are unavailable.
