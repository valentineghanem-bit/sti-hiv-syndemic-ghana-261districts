Background

Ghana bears a dual burden of HIV (national prevalence ~1.7%) and curable sexually transmitted infections (STIs), with marked geographic heterogeneity across its 260 health districts. STIs amplify HIV transmission risk two- to fivefold through mucosal disruption and immune activation, yet spatially explicit, district-level evidence on syndemic co-concentration remains absent in the Ghanaian literature.[1,2]

Syndemic theory posits that co-occurring epidemics, mediated by shared social determinants, produce excess burden exceeding their independent effects. Identifying spatial co-clustering of HIV and STI prevalence is therefore critical for prioritising geographically targeted prevention resources.[3]

Objectives: (1) Quantify spatial autocorrelation of HIV and STI burden; (2) identify bivariate co-cluster districts; (3) determine behavioural determinants using interpretable machine learning; (4) model district-level risk via spatial lag regression.

Methods

Data sources: Ghana DHS 2014 (HIV biomarker), DHS 2022 (behavioural indicators), Census 2021 (district population denominators), WHO GHO 2022 (ANC syphilis seroprevalence). District-level estimates derived by model-based small-area estimation from regional DHS strata using Census 2021 auxiliary covariates (poverty index; literacy rate).[4]

Syndemic Burden Index (SBI): Composite of MinMax-normalised HIV prevalence and STI incidence, scaled 0–10.

Spatial statistics: Global Moran's I and Bivariate LISA (Queen contiguity + KNN-5 for island districts; 999 permutations). K-means clustering (k=4, silhouette coefficient).

Machine learning: XGBoost (100 estimators; max_depth=4) with native SHAP interpretability; leave-one-region-out spatial cross-validation (9 folds). Spatial lag regression (OLS + spatial lag term).[5]

Conclusions

HIV and STI distributions in Ghana are not random — both exhibit strong positive spatial autocorrelation (Moran's I >0.5) and significant bivariate co-clustering, with 35 High-High hotspot districts requiring immediate syndemic intervention.

Higher-risk sexual behaviour is the primary modifiable determinant; condom use exerts a protective spatial effect. The Critical Syndemic Burden cluster (n=48 districts) displays HIV prevalence 6.5× higher than the Low Risk cluster, representing the priority tier for geographically targeted prevention resources.

Limitations: Ecological design; district estimates are model-derived from regional DHS data; ecological fallacy precludes individual-level inference.

Policy Implications

Confirmed HIV×STI hotspot districts trigger obligations under:

IHR 2005 ICESCR Art.12 Ghana Act 851 Ghana Act 852

Priority actions: (1) district-targeted condom distribution in 35 HH hotspot districts; (2) VCT scale-up in Critical Syndemic Burden cluster; (3) mandatory STI notification enforcement (Act 851 §14) in districts with DHIMS2 reporting completeness <80%; (4) NHIF package review for STI services in under-resourced hotspot districts.
